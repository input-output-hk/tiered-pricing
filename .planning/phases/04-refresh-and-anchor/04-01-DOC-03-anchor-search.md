## Per-knob anchor decisions

### Sub-knob 1: Window length 32 (capacity-varying signals)

- **Disposition:** ANCHORED
- **Citations consulted:**
  - Reijsbergen, Sridhar, Monnot, Leonardos et al., *"Transaction Fees on a Honeymoon: Ethereum's EIP-1559 One Month Later"*, AFT 2021 (also IEEE ICBC 2022; DLT R&P 2025 follow-up) — outcome: **anchor-supporting**. Reports "intense, chaotic oscillations in block sizes and slow adjustments during periods of demand bursts" under EIP-1559's per-block, no-smoothing controller; proposes AIMD (additive-increase / multiplicative-decrease) with a variable learning rate as a fix. Motivates the *kind* of choice phase-2 makes (any smoothing layer beyond the unwindowed Ethereum baseline; phase-2 picks a capacity-weighted window over AIMD).
  - Leonardos, Reijsbergen, Monnot et al., *"Dynamical analysis of the EIP-1559 Ethereum fee market"*, AFT 2021 — outcome: **anchor-supporting**. Gives the theoretical bounded-oscillation result under variable-demand uniform-bidder model. Motivates a smoothing layer as the standard remedial response in the EIP-1559 academic critique tradition.
  - Liu, Lu, Nayak, Zhang, Zhang, Zhao, *"Empirical Analysis of EIP-1559: Transaction Fees, Waiting Times, and Consensus Security"*, CCS 2022 — outcome: **anchor-supporting as counter-bound**. Reports that "concerns raised by Reijsbergen et al. relating to base-fee volatility making fee estimation more difficult does not hold in practice" on Ethereum mainnet. Frames the motivation honestly: phase-2's smoothing is a defensive choice against a theoretical risk that has not yet manifested as a Ethereum production failure.
  - Azouvi, Goren, Heimbach, Hicks, *"Base Fee Manipulation in Ethereum's EIP-1559 Transaction Fee Mechanism"*, DISC 2023 — outcome: **secondary-anchor-supporting**. Documents a 20%-minority empty-block-then-half-full base-fee manipulation attack; window smoothing weakens this attack vector because the attacker must sustain the strategy across the window length, not just the parent block. Not the primary motivation, but a real ancillary benefit the disclosure paragraph can name.
  - `docs/phase-2/mechanism-design.md` §"Common design choices" lines 97–103 (in-repo) — outcome: **architectural-motivation-supporting**. The spec walks the alternatives (immediate per-block fill rate, per-RB settlement-step aggregation, exponentially-weighted moving average over per-block utilisations, median over per-block utilisations) and rules each out for the linear-Leios block-mix where ranking blocks (RBs ~90 KiB) are blended with endorser blocks (EBs up to 12 MiB) at a 133× capacity ratio. The capacity-weighted-window choice is the spec-internal response that drives the same problem as the EIP-1559 literature but with a different architectural answer.

- **Decision rationale:** Under the D-35 motivating-citation bar — a published reference motivates *the kind of choice* the simulator makes — the EIP-1559 literature directly motivates introducing a smoothing layer beyond the unwindowed Ethereum baseline. The literature's preferred remedial response is AIMD; phase-2's response is a capacity-weighted window of length 32. The literature does not directly motivate the *specific* value 32 (it remains a round-number choice between unwindowed and over-smoothed regimes), but D-35 explicitly accepts kind-of-choice motivation without a numerical match. The `phase-2-eip1559-smoothing` suite sweeps {16, 32, 64} for sensitivity; this is a within-simulator robustness check rather than an external anchor, but it is the right ancillary disclosure to pair with the literature citation.

### Sub-knob 2: Multiplier-floor 4 (used in `phase-2-rb-scarcity` and `phase-2-urgency-inversion`)

- **Disposition:** DISCLOSED
- **Citations consulted:**
  - Liu et al. CCS 2022; Reijsbergen et al. AFT 2021; Leonardos et al. AFT 2021 — outcome: **all rejected as multiplier-floor anchors**. Each analyses single-lane Ethereum Improvement Proposal 1559 (EIP-1559). Ethereum has no second-lane multiplier floor at all, so the kind of choice the simulator makes (a numerical price-discrimination ratio between standard and priority lanes) has no Ethereum analogue.
  - `docs/phase-2/mechanism-design.md` line 155 + line 290 (in-repo) — outcome: **internal motivation only**. The mechanism-design specification names 16 as the default and treats the floor as configurable, with the justification "gives a strong price-discrimination guarantee". The specification does not cite empirical economic evidence for either 4 or 16, and the *kind* of choice (a floor magnitude that guarantees price discrimination) has no published external rationale.
  - `CLAUDE.md` §"Calibration choices" — outcome: **internal motivation only**. Records the empirical observation that at the spec default 16, only urgency≥5 actor components self-select into the priority lane and priority demand stays too thin to surface controller drift; at 4, urgency≥2 components pick priority and the controller drifts. This is an internal calibration-accommodation story driven by the simulator's own actor model (utility-maximising lane choice via `libm::pow` and `libm::round` into integer lovelace), not a published external anchor.
  - `.planning/realism-tests/multiplier-floor-16-companion/results.md` (TEST-07a; in-repo) — outcome: **regime-dependence evidence, not an anchor**. Found that at floor = 16, the `phase-2-rb-scarcity` welfare finding inverts and the `phase-2-urgency-inversion` finding weakly reverses. This evidences the calibration's regime-dependence but does not anchor the value 4.

- **Decision rationale:** The kind of choice — a magnitude for a second-lane price-discrimination ratio — has no published external anchor under D-35: Ethereum has no second-lane mechanism, no other deployed dynamic-pricing system with a comparable controller has a comparable knob, and the EIP-1559 academic critique tradition does not extend to second-lane controllers because no such second-lane controller is deployed. The 4 value is an internal calibration accommodation (named as such in `CLAUDE.md` §"Calibration choices" and the `phase-2-rb-scarcity` and `phase-2-urgency-inversion` suite READMEs); the regime-dependence evidence at floor = 16 (TEST-07a) narrows the welfare claim further. This is the canonical "conditional on X" disclosure case per D-35: no external anchor exists; the value is disclosed with the conditional frame and the suites covering the spec default 16 (`phase-2-priority-only-rb-reserved`, `phase-2-priority-only-unreserved`, `phase-2-two-lane-both-dynamic`) cited as the cross-suite robustness check.

### Sub-knob 4: Lane-signal-source choices

- **Disposition:** DISCLOSED
- **Citations consulted:**
  - Liu et al. CCS 2022; Reijsbergen et al. AFT 2021; Leonardos et al. AFT 2021 — outcome: **all rejected as lane-signal-source anchors**. None of the three Ethereum-side citations address a second-lane signal source because Ethereum has no second lane. They establish the single-lane EIP-1559 signal source (parent-block gas-used against parent-block gas-target) but cannot motivate any of the three open candidates for un-reserved priority signal source (option 1: priority-paying-bytes share of total block capacity; option 2: priority-paying-bytes against a notional priority share; option 3: observed delay-gap signal) in `docs/phase-2/mechanism-design.md` lines 207–211.
  - `docs/phase-2/mechanism-design.md` lines 207–211 + line 238 (in-repo) — outcome: **explicitly leaves the choice open**. The mechanism-design specification enumerates three candidate signal sources for un-reserved priority and states "The decision is left to a follow-up design pass". Line 238 raises the same open question for the both-dynamic standard signal source ("standard-fee bytes against what denominator?"). The specification cannot serve as its own anchor for a choice it explicitly defers.
  - Roughgarden, *"Transaction Fee Mechanism Design for the Ethereum Blockchain"* (EC 2021 / working paper) — outcome: **rejected as a lane-signal-source anchor**. Analyses single-lane EIP-1559's incentive properties (myopic-miner-incentive-compatibility (MMIC), off-chain-agreement (OCA)-proofness, dominant-strategy-incentive-compatibility (DSIC) except under demand spike). Does not consider a second-lane controller architecture and therefore does not motivate any lane-signal-source choice.
  - Sui / Solana / NEAR deployed-system documentation (in Spike 003 §"Other deployed dynamic-fee mechanisms (for triangulation)") — outcome: **rejected as lane-signal-source anchors**. Sui uses epoch-boundary validator-governance voting on a single reference gas price (no second lane). Solana uses user-side priority-fee bids with no controller (no second lane in the closed-loop sense). NEAR adjusts minimum gas with token price plus sharding (different paradigm; no second lane). None has a comparable lane-signal-source choice.

- **Decision rationale:** D-35 explicitly flags this sub-knob as "likely stays DISCLOSED" because the simulator picks option 1 of three spec-open candidates without external evidence. The consulted citations confirm the prediction: the EIP-1559 literature does not extend to second-lane signal sources, and no deployed dynamic-pricing system has a comparable second-lane signal-source choice. Per D-37 the marginal new citation is judged unlikely to anchor this sub-knob from a 2024–2026 follow-up pass, because anchoring would require either (a) a deployed system with a comparable second-lane signal-source choice (none exists) or (b) an academic paper specifically analysing the three-candidate space in `docs/phase-2/mechanism-design.md` lines 207–211 (no such paper would exist; the specification is phase-2-internal). The "conditional on X" disclosure frame applies cleanly: welfare findings from the un-reserved priority arm and the both-dynamic standard side are conditional on the specific signal-source definitions chosen.
